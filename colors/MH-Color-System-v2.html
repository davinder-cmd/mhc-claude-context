Mobile Health — Color System v2

The v1 was too safe. This version pushes harder on where color actually lives in the interface — colored surfaces, domain-coded cards, bolder header treatments, and proper warmth. The palette is the same; the conviction is higher.

The Problem

Why the current app feels bland

Four structural issues that no hex code change alone can fix. The palette matters, but where and how much color appears matters more.

1. White-on-white flatness

Every surface is white or near-white. Cards sit on white backgrounds with 1px gray borders. There's no visual depth, no layering, no hierarchy between the page and the content on it. Everything floats in the same blank plane.

Fix: Tinted app background + white cards

The app background shifts to a subtle blue-tinted off-white (#F5F8FB). Cards pop against it. Some cards get domain-specific tinted backgrounds (teal-50 for health, apricot-50 for rewards). Depth returns through color, not drop shadows.

2. Single-point-of-color syndrome

The only color in the entire app is one cyan button and the active nav item. Everything else is black text on white. The app has no color personality — it could be any product in any industry.

Fix: Distributed color moments

Color appears at 5–7 points per screen: the header gradient, category-coded icon backgrounds, progress bar fills, chip/pill treatments, tinted card surfaces, and the warm accent for rewards. Each has a clear purpose — never decorative.

3. Photography dependency

Stock photos are carrying the entire visual load. Remove them and the screen is a wireframe. The interface has no identity without borrowed imagery.

Fix: Color-as-content

Domain-colored avatars, tinted surfaces, and illustrated icons give each card its own identity. Photography becomes enhancement, not crutch. The app is recognizable at a glance even without images.

4. Zero warmth or personality

The cool gray neutrals + clinical cyan make the app feel like a form you fill out, not a companion on your health journey. There's no emotional warmth anywhere. It feels institutional.

Fix: Warm accent presence + teal vitality

Apricot warmth appears in rewards, streaks, and celebration moments. Teal brings life energy to health data and progress. Lavender softens mental health touchpoints. The app feels like it cares about you.

Usage rules

Where each color earns its place

Every accent color has a specific job. They never appear randomly. This is what turns a palette into a system.
Ocean Blue — Structure

Header gradients, primary buttons, active navigation, links, focus rings. The backbone of every screen. Where the eye goes first for action.

Sea Glass Teal — Vitality

Health data, activity progress, fitness domain, wellness scores, step counters. Anything about your body being active and alive.

Warm Apricot — Reward

Points, streaks, badges, gamification, celebration moments, premium features. The warm payoff that makes engagement feel good.

Soft Lavender — Mind

Mental health, sleep, mindfulness, therapy, emotional coaching, AI insights. The gentle, introspective moments in the experience.

Sage — Nourish

Nutrition, diet, recipes, meal tracking, preventive care, wellness programs. The "tending your garden" moments.

Neutral — Foundation

Everything structural: body text, borders, dividers, disabled states, backgrounds. The quiet scaffold that lets the other colors sing.

The 60-30-10 rule for any screen

60%
Neutrals + surfaces
White cards, off-white background, gray text, borders. The breathable canvas.
30%
Primary blue
Header, buttons, active states, links. Structural color that guides action.
10%
Domain accents
Teal, apricot, lavender, sage. Appear only in their domain context. Never random.
What changed from v1

v1 → v2 changelog

Palette (unchanged)
Same hex values. The colors were never the problem — the conviction was. Every token, ramp, and accessibility finding from v1 still applies.
Usage (overhauled)
Gradient header replaces flat white bar. Tinted app background (#F5F8FB) replaces pure white. Domain-coded card surfaces (lavender for mental health, apricot for rewards, teal for activity). Sparkline data viz uses domain palette. 60-30-10 distribution enforced. Photography dependency removed — color carries identity alone.
