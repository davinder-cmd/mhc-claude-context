# Product Design Experts — Reference

People who shaped how we think about what to build, why to build it, and how to ship it. Use these references when making strategic product decisions, not just evaluating usability or visual quality.

---

## Tier 1: Foundational Thinkers

These defined modern product thinking.

### Marty Cagan
**Known for:** *Inspired*, *Empowered*, Silicon Valley Product Group, product discovery

**Why he matters:** Definitive voice on how great product teams work. His distinction between "feature teams" (output) and "empowered teams" (outcomes) is the benchmark for product organization maturity.

**Core concepts:**
- **Product discovery:** Validate before you build
- **Empowered teams:** Teams own problems, not feature requests
- **Continuous discovery:** Always learning, not just at project start
- **Product trio:** PM + Design + Engineering working together

**Best content:**
- *Inspired* (book) — how tech companies create products customers love
- *Empowered* (book) — building empowered product teams
- svpg.com articles — tactical product management guidance

**Follow:** svpg.com, @caborynagan

**What to learn:** Product discovery, team structure, outcome-oriented product development

---

### Julie Zhuo
**Known for:** *The Making of a Manager*, VP Design at Facebook/Meta, design leadership

**Why she matters:** Best contemporary voice on design leadership and scaling design organizations. Practical, honest about what's hard.

**Core concepts:**
- Design managers make other designers better
- Feedback is a gift — give it clearly and often
- Great design comes from great teams, not heroic individuals

**Best content:**
- *The Making of a Manager* (book) — design leadership essentials
- The Looking Glass (Substack) — weekly essays on product and design
- "How to Work with Designers" — classic article

**Follow:** @joulee, lg.substack.com

**What to learn:** Design management, giving feedback, scaling design teams

---

### Clayton Christensen
**Known for:** Jobs to Be Done, *The Innovator's Dilemma*, disruptive innovation

**Why he matters:** "Jobs to Be Done" reframes product thinking from features to customer progress. Instead of "what features do users want," ask "what job are they hiring this product to do?"

**Core concepts:**
- **Jobs to Be Done:** People "hire" products to make progress
- **Disruptive innovation:** Why incumbents lose to upstarts
- **Outcome-driven innovation:** Measure success by customer progress

**Best content:**
- *Competing Against Luck* (book) — Jobs to Be Done applied
- *The Innovator's Dilemma* (book) — disruption theory
- "Milkshake Marketing" — classic JTBD case study

**What to learn:** JTBD framework, customer progress, why features aren't strategy

## Tier 2: Execution & Leadership

These shaped how products actually get built and shipped.

### Scott Belsky
**Known for:** *The Messy Middle*, Behance founder, Adobe CPO

**Why he matters:** Honest about the grind between starting and finishing. Most product advice focuses on ideation or launch — he focuses on the 90% in between.

**Core concepts:**
- The messy middle is where most projects die
- Endurance > brilliance for shipping
- Optimize for the team's energy, not just the roadmap

**Best content:**
- *The Messy Middle* (book) — surviving the long slog of execution
- *Making Ideas Happen* (book) — turning ideas into reality

**Follow:** @scottbelsky

**What to learn:** Execution endurance, team energy management, shipping through uncertainty

---

### Jake Knapp
**Known for:** Design Sprints, *Sprint*, GV (Google Ventures)

**Why he matters:** Created the most widely-adopted rapid validation framework. Design Sprints compress months of debate into a week of focused work.

**The Sprint structure:**
- Monday: Map the problem
- Tuesday: Sketch solutions
- Wednesday: Decide
- Thursday: Prototype
- Friday: Test with real users

**Best content:**
- *Sprint* (book) — the complete framework
- *Make Time* (book) — personal productivity
- thesprintbook.com — resources and templates

**Follow:** @jakaborynapp

**What to learn:** Rapid validation, time-boxed decision making, prototype-to-test cycles

---

### Ryan Singer
**Known for:** *Shape Up*, Basecamp, appetite-driven development

**Why he matters:** Alternative to Agile/Scrum that many find more humane. "Appetite" (how much time is this worth?) instead of "estimate" (how long will this take?).

**Core concepts:**
- **Shaping:** Define the problem boundaries before building
- **Appetite:** Fixed time, variable scope
- **Six-week cycles:** Long enough to build, short enough to stay focused
- **Cool-down:** Buffer between cycles for exploration

**Best content:**
- *Shape Up* (book, free online) — basecamp.com/shapeup
- "Appetite vs. Estimates" — why fixed-time works

**What to learn:** Shaping work, appetite-driven planning, alternatives to Scrum

---

### Teresa Torres
**Known for:** Continuous discovery, opportunity solution trees, *Continuous Discovery Habits*

**Why she matters:** Most practical framework for ongoing product discovery. Opportunity Solution Trees make discovery work visible and structured.

**Core concepts:**
- **Continuous discovery:** Weekly customer touchpoints, not quarterly research
- **Opportunity Solution Trees:** Visual framework connecting outcomes → opportunities → solutions
- **Compare & contrast:** Test assumptions by comparing solutions

**Best content:**
- *Continuous Discovery Habits* (book) — the complete framework
- producttalk.org — articles and templates

**Follow:** @ttorres, producttalk.org

**What to learn:** Continuous discovery habits, opportunity mapping, assumption testing

These shaped how we think about product strategy and market positioning.

### Gibson Biddle
**Known for:** Netflix VP Product, DHM model, product strategy frameworks

**Why he matters:** Best frameworks for thinking about product strategy — not just what to build, but why it wins.

**Core concepts:**
- **DHM Model:** Delight customers, in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing ways

- **Strategy = focus:** What you won't do is as important as what you will

**Best content:**
- "How to Define Your Product Strategy" (essays) — gibsonbiddle.medium.com

## Frameworks Quick Reference

### Jobs to Be Done (Christensen)
- What progress is the customer trying to make?
- What's the struggling moment that triggers "hiring" a solution?
- What are they firing when they hire you?

### Opportunity Solution Trees (Torres)
```
Outcome (business goal)
└── Opportunity (customer need/pain)
    ├── Solution A
    │   └── Experiment
    └── Solution B
        └── Experiment
```

### DHM Model (Biddle)
- **Delight:** Does it create customer joy?
- **Hard-to-copy:** Is there a moat?
- **Margin-enhancing:** Does it make money?

### LNO Framework (Doshi)
- **Leverage:** 10x impact, do excellently
- **Neutral:** Competent is enough
- **Overhead:** Minimize time spent

### Shape Up Cycle (Singer)
1. Shape (define boundaries) — 2 weeks
2. Build (execute) — 6 weeks
3. Cool-down (breathe, explore) — 2 weeks

### Design Sprint (Knapp)
Mon → Map | Tue → Sketch | Wed → Decide | Thu → Prototype | Fri → Test

## How to Use This Reference

### When deciding what to build:
1. Apply Jobs to Be Done — what progress does the customer want?
2. Use Opportunity Solution Trees to map possibilities
3. Check against DHM — will this delight, be hard to copy, and make money?

### When planning execution:
1. Consider Shape Up's appetite model — fixed time, variable scope
2. Use Design Sprints for high-uncertainty decisions
3. Apply LNO to prioritize what deserves excellence

### When validating ideas:
1. Teresa Torres' continuous discovery habits
2. Cagan's product discovery techniques
3. Design Sprint prototype + test cycle

### When positioning the product:
1. April Dunford's positioning framework
2. Define competitive alternatives
3. Clarify the struggling moment you solve
