# UX & Usability Experts — Reference

People who shaped how we think about usability, user experience, and human-centered design. Use these references when evaluating whether something *works* for users, not just whether it looks right.

---

## Tier 1: Foundational Thinkers

These defined the field. Their frameworks are the vocabulary of UX.

### Jakob Nielsen
**Known for:** Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics, Nielsen Norman Group, discount usability methods

**Why he matters:** His 10 heuristics are the universal checklist for evaluating interface usability. If you're reviewing a design for usability problems, you're using his framework whether you know it or not.

**The 10 Heuristics:**
1. Visibility of system status
2. Match between system and real world
3. User control and freedom
4. Consistency and standards
5. Error prevention
6. Recognition rather than recall
7. Flexibility and efficiency of use
8. Aesthetic and minimalist design
9. Help users recognize, diagnose, recover from errors
10. Help and documentation

**Best content:**
- *Usability Engineering* (book) — foundational text
- nngroup.com/articles — hundreds of evidence-based articles
- "10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design" — the canonical reference

**Follow:** nngroup.com, @NNgroup

**What to learn:** Heuristic evaluation, usability testing methods, evidence-based design decisions

---

### Don Norman
**Known for:** Coined "User Experience," *The Design of Everyday Things*, affordances, signifiers

**Why he matters:** He defined how we think about the relationship between humans and designed objects. "Affordance" and "signifier" are his vocabulary. If a door handle confuses people, that's a Norman problem.

**Best content:**
- *The Design of Everyday Things* (book) — required reading, explains why bad design happens
- *Emotional Design* (book) — why aesthetics matter for usability
- jnd.org — essays on design thinking

**Follow:** jnd.org, @jnd1er

**What to learn:** Affordances, signifiers, conceptual models, why users blame themselves for bad design

---

### Steve Krug
**Known for:** *Don't Make Me Think*, guerrilla usability testing, "trunk test"

**Why he matters:** Made usability accessible to non-specialists. His "don't make me think" principle is the simplest usability test: if users have to think about how to use it, it's too complicated.

**Best content:**
- *Don't Make Me Think* (book) — the most readable usability book ever written
- *Rocket Surgery Made Easy* (book) — DIY usability testing guide
- "The Trunk Test" — can users figure out where they are and how to navigate?

**Follow:** sensible.com

**What to learn:** Guerrilla testing, simplicity as a design goal, practical usability evaluation

## Tier 2: Research & Strategy

These shaped how we conduct user research and make strategic UX decisions.

### Jared Spool
**Known for:** UIE (User Interface Engineering), design maturity, UX strategy

**Why he matters:** Bridges research and business outcomes. Strong on how to measure UX value and build organizational UX capability.

**Best content:**
- "The $300 Million Button" — classic case study on UX ROI
- UIE articles and podcasts (uie.com)
- Talks on design maturity and organizational change

**Follow:** @jmspool, uie.com

**What to learn:** UX ROI, design maturity models, research that drives decisions

---

### Erika Hall
**Known for:** *Just Enough Research*, Mule Design, research ethics

**Why she matters:** Best thinking on how to do research efficiently without over-engineering it. Strong on asking the right questions and avoiding research theater.

**Best content:**
- *Just Enough Research* (book) — practical research methods
- *Conversational Design* (book) — designing for dialogue
- A List Apart articles

**Follow:** @mulegirl, muledesign.com

**What to learn:** Research planning, stakeholder interviews, avoiding research that doesn't inform decisions

### Aarron Walter
**Known for:** *Designing for Emotion*, emotional design hierarchy, formerly MailChimp

**Why he matters:** Extended Norman's emotional design thinking into practical application. His hierarchy (functional → reliable → usable → pleasurable) is a useful maturity model.

**Best content:**
- *Designing for Emotion* (book) — why delight matters after you nail basics

**Follow:** @aarron

**What to learn:** Emotional design, personality in interfaces, when to add delight (and when not to)

## Heuristics & Frameworks Quick Reference

### Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics
Use for: Evaluating any interface for basic usability problems

| # | Heuristic | Quick Check |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Visibility of system status | Does the user know what's happening? |
| 2 | Match system & real world | Does it use familiar language/concepts? |
| 3 | User control & freedom | Can they undo, exit, go back? |
| 4 | Consistency & standards | Does it work like similar products? |
| 5 | Error prevention | Does it prevent mistakes before they happen? |
| 6 | Recognition over recall | Can they see options vs. remember them? |
| 7 | Flexibility & efficiency | Are there shortcuts for experts? |
| 8 | Aesthetic & minimalist | Is everything necessary? |
| 9 | Help with errors | Are error messages helpful and specific? |
| 10 | Help & documentation | Is help available when needed? |

### Norman's Design Principles
- **Affordances:** What actions does the design suggest?
- **Signifiers:** What cues tell users what to do?
- **Mapping:** Does the relationship between controls and outcomes make sense?
- **Feedback:** Does the system communicate what's happening?
- **Conceptual Model:** Can users build an accurate mental model?

### Krug's "Don't Make Me Think" Tests
- **Trunk Test:** Drop someone in the middle — can they navigate?
- **Billboard Test:** Can users get the point in 3 seconds?
- **Squint Test:** Does the visual hierarchy work when blurred?

### Walter's Emotional Design Hierarchy
1. **Functional** — Does it work?
2. **Reliable** — Does it work consistently?
3. **Usable** — Is it easy to use?
4. **Pleasurable** — Is it enjoyable?

(Don't pursue level 4 until levels 1-3 are solid)

## How to Use This Reference

### When evaluating a design for usability:
1. Run through Nielsen's 10 heuristics
2. Apply Krug's trunk/billboard/squint tests
3. Check Norman's affordances and signifiers

### When designing forms or onboarding:
1. Start with Luke Wroblewski's form patterns
2. Reference Caroline Jarrett for question wording
3. Test with Krug's guerrilla methods

### When building personas:
1. Use Kim Goodwin's goal-directed framework
2. Ground in real research, not assumptions
3. Focus on goals and behaviors, not demographics

### When justifying UX investment:
1. Jared Spool on UX ROI
2. Nielsen's discount usability methods (cheap testing > no testing)
3. Evidence from nngroup.com studies
